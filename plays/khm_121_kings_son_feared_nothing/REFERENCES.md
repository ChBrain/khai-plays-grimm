# The King's Son Who Feared Nothing: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Der Königssohn, der sich vor nichts fürchtete_ (KHM 121) as a heroic disenchantment tale: a fearless prince who courts every danger, is betrayed and blinded by a giant, restored by a faithful lion, and at last frees an enchanted princess by enduring three nights of torment in silence. The production models how unwavering courage and silent steadfastness outlast treachery and break a spell.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_ (1812-1857), tale KHM 121, in the definitive 7th edition of 1857.

| Source             | Key Work / Event                   | Scope                                                                                                                                         |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Brothers Grimm** | _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_, KHM 121 | The canonical tale of the king's son who feared nothing, the giant's betrayal, the faithful lion, and the disenchantment of the black maiden. |

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Anatomy of blinding and healing**: the loss and return of the prince's sight are treated as narrative state changes, not as medical or biological process.
- **Mechanism of enchantment**: the spell on the castle and the maiden is modeled as a binding condition broken by silence and the threefold sword-stroke, without any underlying magic system.
- **Cross-play context**: strict local isolation. Every link stays within this play's directory.
