# Brother and Sister: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Bruederchen und Schwesterchen_ (KHM 011) as a single long scheme of envy that succeeds at its cruelties and is then undone. The production models the tale not as a string of enchantments but as one witch-stepmother's design to destroy the fled siblings: curse the brother through the forest springs, then infiltrate the castle, smother the young queen, and substitute her own one-eyed daughter in the king's bed. The spring-water, the garter, and the bath are the load-bearing instruments of that scheme, which takes its victims before the murdered queen's returning ghost turns it all back.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmaerchen_ (1812-1857), tale KHM 011, in the definitive 7th edition of 1857.

| Source             | Key Work / Event                    | Scope                                                                |
| ------------------ | ----------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Brothers Grimm** | _Kinder- und Hausmaerchen_, KHM 011 | The canonical tale of the roebuck brother and the substituted bride. |

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The curse as accident**: the brother's transformation is modeled as the first move of a deliberate plan, the stepmother's enchantment of the springs, not as random misfortune.
- **Clean villainy defeated by virtue alone**: the witch's scheme genuinely succeeds at murder and substitution; its reversal is the work of the returning queen, not a tidy moral guarantee.
- **The substitution undetected**: the one-eyed daughter in the king's bed is encoded as a real, momentarily successful deception that the ghost's return exposes.
- **Cross-play context**: strict local isolation. Every link stays within this play's directory.
