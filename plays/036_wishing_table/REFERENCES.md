# The Wishing-Table: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Tischchen deck dich, Goldesel und Knueppel aus dem Sack_ (KHM 036) as a collision of two schemes at one inn: a thieving swap against a baited cudgel. The production models the tale not as a string of magic gifts but as a strict contest of plans, the innkeeper's design to swap each brother's wonder-gift for a worthless copy in his sleep set against the youngest son's design to bait the thief with the cudgel-in-the-sack and beat the stolen gifts back out of him. The wishing-table, the gold-donkey, and the cudgel are load-bearing instruments in that contest, and the outcome is decided not by honest craft alone but by who out-schemes whom.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmaerchen_ (1812-1857), tale KHM 036, in the definitive 7th edition of 1857.

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Sanitized theft**: the innkeeper's repeated swaps are modeled as the cold engine that strips the first two brothers, not softened away.
- **Accidental recovery**: the return of the gifts is not luck. It is the work of [die Knueppelfalle des Juengsten](plan_cudgel-trap.md), a deliberate trap whose steps succeed in sequence.
- **Cross-play context**: strict local isolation. Every link stays within this play's directory.
