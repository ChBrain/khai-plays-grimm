# St. Joseph in the Forest: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Der heilige Joseph im Walde_ (KHM 201) as a study of the heart tested by hospitality and the divine measuring of charity against greed. The production models a covetous mother who treats her best and most pious child harshly, and three daughters sent out in turn. Each loses her way in the forest, finds the hut of the holy old man St. Joseph, and is judged by how she shares his meager root soup: the youngest gives all and is rewarded with a heavy bag of money, the middle daughter shares fairly and keeps only a few coins, and the greedy eldest refuses to share and is punished with a second nose. The tale closes on the doom of greed, as mother and eldest daughter, searching for money, are killed by snakes and lizards.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_ (1812-1857), tale KHM 201, one of the Children's Legends (Kinderlegenden) that close the collection, here referenced under its German title "Der heilige Joseph im Walde".

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Theology of sainthood**: St. Joseph is treated as a plain narrative tester and giver, not as a doctrinal figure; his holiness is a function, not a creed.
- **The mechanism of the reward and punishment**: the heavy money bag, the second nose, and the deadly snakes and lizards are modeled as narrative tokens of just measure, not as physical or biological events.
- **Cross-play context**: strict local isolation. Every link stays within this play's directory.
