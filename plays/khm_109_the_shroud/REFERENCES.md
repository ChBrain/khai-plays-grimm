# The Shroud: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Das Totenhemdchen_ (KHM 109) as a quiet study of grief and release. The production models how a mourner's boundless tears keep the dead from rest, and how the surrender of sorrow to God grants both the child its grave-rest and the mother her peace. The drying shroud serves as the single visible measure of that release.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_ (1812-1857), tale KHM 109, in the definitive 7th edition of 1857.

| Source             | Key Work / Event                   | Scope                                                                   |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Brothers Grimm** | _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_, KHM 109 | The canonical tale "Das Totenhemdchen" of the dead child and the tears. |

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Theology of the afterlife**: God taking the child and granting rest is treated as a given premise of the tale, not a doctrine to be elaborated or argued.
- **Cause of the illness**: the child's sudden sickness and death are modeled as a single state transition, without medical detail or explanation.
- **Cross-play context**: strict local isolation. Every link stays within this play's directory.
