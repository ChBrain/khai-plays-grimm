# The Dog and the Sparrow: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Der Hund und der Sperling_ (KHM 058) as a single deliberate scheme: the sparrow's measured revenge. The production models the tale not as a beast fable but as one escalating design of retribution. After the wagoner deliberately drives over the sleeping dog, the loyal sparrow ruins him by degrees: he spills all the wine, blinds the horses, lets the birds devour the grain, and goads the man into the self-struck blows that kill him. The wine cask, the wagon, and the axe are the load-bearing instruments of that revenge, which succeeds at every step and ends in the wagoner's self-destruction.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmaerchen_ (1812-1857), tale KHM 058, in the definitive 7th edition of 1857.

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Sanitized vengeance**: the deliberate murder of the dog and the ruin and death visited on the wagoner are modeled as the cold logic of the plan, not softened into a gentle lesson.
- **Accidental ruin**: the wagoner's downfall is not chance. It is the worked-through result of [die Rache des Sperlings](plan_sparrows-revenge.md), a single plan whose steps land in sequence.
- **Cross-play context**: strict local isolation. Every link stays within this play's directory.
