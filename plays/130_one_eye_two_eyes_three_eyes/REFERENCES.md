# One-Eye, Two-Eyes, and Three-Eyes: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Einäuglein, Zweiäuglein und Dreiäuglein_ (KHM 130) as a collision of two schemes within one cruel household: envy against deliverance. The production models the tale not as a Cinderella variant of pity and luck but as a strict contest of plans, the mother and sisters' design to spy out and destroy the goat that feeds the despised girl set against Two-Eyes' design, given by the wise woman, to bury the goat's remains, raise a tree that obeys her alone, and win the passing knight. The third eye, the slaughtered goat, the silver-and-gold tree, and the cask are the load-bearing instruments, and the outcome is decided not by who is favored but by which plan outlasts the other.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_ (1812-1857), tale KHM 130, in the definitive 7th edition of 1857.

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Passive victimhood**: Two-Eyes' rescue is not luck or mere pity. It is the run of [die Erlösung durch die weise Frau](plan_silver-tree.md), a deliberate counter-plan whose targets all close as successes.
- **Motiveless cruelty**: the family's malice is modeled as the working scheme [das Aushungern des Zweiäugleins](plan_starve-the-favored.md), a plan that wins its early targets and is then overtaken, not as random spite.
- **Cross-play context**: strict local isolation. Every link stays within this play's directory.
