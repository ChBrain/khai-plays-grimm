# The Fox and the Geese: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Der Fuchs und die Gänse_ (KHM 086) as an illustration of how the seemingly defenseless escape an overwhelming predator by exploiting a condition he himself grants. The production models the contrast between the Fox's raw, certain power over his prey and the flock's single weapon, a last prayer whose fulfillment can be stretched into infinity, so that the granted reprieve suspends the sentence forever.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_ (1812-1857), tale KHM 086, in the definitive 7th edition of 1857.

| Source             | Key Work / Event                   | Scope                                                              |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Brothers Grimm** | _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_, KHM 086 | The canonical teasing tale of the fox and the never-ending prayer. |

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Theology of the prayer**: the last prayer is treated as a formal, granted condition, ignoring any doctrinal content beyond the endless "Ga! Ga!".
- **Counting the flock**: the geese are modeled as one persona plus a spokesgoose, rather than individually enumerated birds.
- **Cross-play context**: strict local isolation. Every link stays within this play's directory.
