# The Devil with the Three Golden Hairs: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Der Teufel mit den drei goldenen Haaren_ (KHM 029) as a collision of two schemes around a prophecy: a king's serial murder against a luck-child's quest. The production models the tale not as a simple fortune fable but as a strict contest of plans, the king's design to drown, letter, and damn the prophesied child set against the child's design to fetch the three golden hairs and three answers from hell by hiding while the Devil's grandmother plucks them. The box, the death-letter, the hairs, the riddles, and the answers are load-bearing instruments in that contest, and the outcome is decided not by power but by which design the prophecy carries.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmaerchen_ (1812-1857), tale KHM 029, in the definitive 7th edition of 1857.

| Source             | Key Work / Event                    | Scope                                                                  |
| ------------------ | ----------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Brothers Grimm** | _Kinder- und Hausmaerchen_, KHM 029 | The canonical tale of the luck-child, the death-letter, and the hairs. |

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Sanitized murder**: the king's repeated attempts to kill a child are modeled as the cold engine of the plot, not softened away.
- **Accidental fortune**: the child's survival is not blind luck. It is the prophecy carried by [die Hoellenfahrt des Glueckskinds](plan_child-quest.md), a deliberate quest whose steps succeed in sequence with the grandmother as its hinge.
- **Cross-play context**: strict local isolation. Every link stays within this play's directory.
