# The Girl Without Hands: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Das Maedchen ohne Haende_ (KHM 031) as a single scheme: the devil's claim on the miller's pious daughter. The production models the tale not as a passive martyrdom but as one malicious plan and its undoing. The devil buys the girl by a bargain, and when her purity blocks him, he turns from force to forgery, having her hands cut off and then intercepting the king's letters to drive the order that casts her and her child out. The bargain, the silver hands, and the forged letters are the instruments. The scheme wins the mutilation and the exile, then is broken by divine grace that restores her hands and a king who finds her again.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmaerchen_ (1812-1857), tale KHM 031, in the definitive 7th edition of 1857.

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Sanitized mutilation**: the cutting off of the girl's hands and the casting out of mother and child are modeled as the cold work of the devil's scheme, not softened away.
- **Rescue as chance**: her survival and the regrowth of her hands are not luck. They are the work of divine grace and the angel, the force the scheme did not reckon with.
