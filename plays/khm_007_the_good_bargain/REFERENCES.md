# The Good Bargain: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Der gute Handel_ (KHM 007) as a single scheme that turns apparent foolishness into profit. The production models the tale not as a string of comic misadventures but as one stubborn man's design to be paid in coin where he was sentenced to lashes. The peasant who closes contracts with frogs and dogs loses his cow and his money, yet the same dogged literalism becomes a weapon at court: the sentence of five hundred blows, the bought-up creditors, and the borrowed coat are the load-bearing instruments of the one plan that pays.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmaerchen_ (1812-1857), tale KHM 007, in the definitive 7th edition of 1857.

| Source             | Key Work / Event                    | Scope                                                             |
| ------------------ | ----------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Brothers Grimm** | _Kinder- und Hausmaerchen_, KHM 007 | The canonical tale of the peasant, the sold lashes, and the coat. |

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The peasant as mere fool**: his literal bargains with frogs and dogs are modeled as the stubbornness that later becomes leverage, not as simple stupidity.
- **Lucky reversal**: the peasant's gold is not chance. It is the work of [die Schlaege zu Gold gewendet](plan_lashes-to-gold.md), a deliberate scheme that turns a sentence into a sale.
- **The Jew caricature**: the merchant (Jude) is encoded only as the creditor and the betrayer the plan turns back upon, never as ethnic slander to be reproduced.
- **Cross-play context**: strict local isolation. Every link stays within this play's directory.
