# The Iron Stove: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Der Eisenofen_ (KHM 127) as an illustration of a sworn deliverance undone by a single careless word and regained only through persistent, costly fidelity. The production models the contrast between the binding oath that empowers the rightful deliverer (the princess scraping the stove) and the failed proxies (the miller's and swineherd's daughters), then traces the long second path of search, helper-gifts, and recognition that restores the broken promise.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_ (1812-1857), tale KHM 127, in the definitive 7th edition of 1857.

| Source             | Key Work / Event                   | Scope                                                                            |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Brothers Grimm** | _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_, KHM 127 | The canonical tale of the prince bewitched into an iron stove (_Der Eisenofen_). |

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Mechanics of the witch's spell**: the original enchantment is treated as a fixed precondition with its binding terms (oath, three permitted words), not as a simulated magical process; the witch remains an offstage cause.
- **Geography of the three obstacles**: the glass mountain, the three swords, and the great lake are modeled as discrete gated thresholds each cleared by one gift, ignoring physical terrain or travel detail.
- **Cross-play context**: strict local isolation. Every link stays within this play's directory.
