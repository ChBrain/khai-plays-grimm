# The Sea-Hare: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Das Meerhäschen_ (KHM 191) as an illustration of humble gratitude defeating proud omniscience. The production models how a haughty princess who sees everything through twelve windows beheads every suitor who cannot hide from her, until the youngest of three brothers, who spares a raven, a fish, and a fox on the hunt, is repaid by each in turn and finally hidden as a sea-hare in the princess's own hair, where her all-seeing eye cannot find him.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_ (1812-1857), tale KHM 191, the German title "Das Meerhäschen", in the definitive 7th edition of 1857.

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Optics of the twelve windows**: the princess's all-seeing gaze is treated as a simple search over hiding places, not as a physical or magical optical mechanism.
- **Biology of the transformation**: the spring water and the sea-hare form are modeled as a single state change that yields a hiding place, ignoring any zoology of the marine creature.
- **Cross-play context**: strict local isolation. Every link stays within this play's directory.
