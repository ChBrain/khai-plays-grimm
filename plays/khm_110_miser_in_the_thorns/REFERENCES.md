# The Miser in the Thorns: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging KHM 110 as a study in greed, false witness, and ironic reversal. The production models a content, charitable servant who is rewarded with three irresistible gifts and uses them to wring gold from a covetous figure, who then perjures himself to send the servant to the gallows. The fiddle that compels dancing is the load-bearing instrument: it first extracts the gold in the thorn-hedge and then, on the gallows, dances the whole court until the false accuser confesses the gold was stolen. The covetous false accuser, and not the servant, hangs as a thief.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_ (1812-1857), tale KHM 110, in the definitive 7th edition of 1857.

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The antisemitic caricature**: KHM 110 carries the traditional title "Der Jude im Dorn" ("The Jew Among Thorns") and reproduces an explicit antisemitic stereotype. This production refuses to reproduce that caricature. It deliberately retitles the work "The Miser in the Thorns" and encodes the antagonist solely by his function as [der Geizhals](persona_miser.md), the covetous false accuser whose own purse is exposed as stolen. No ethnic or religious identity, label, slur, or stereotype is staged anywhere; the figure is the greed and the false witness the plan turns back upon, never ethnic slander to be reproduced.
- **The final hanging as endorsement**: the gallows reversal is catalogued as the tale's brutal, ironic structural beat in which a false accuser is destroyed by his own perjury, not as a punishment the production approves or recommends.
- **The dwarf's magic mechanics**: the three gifts are treated as given constraints (an unerring gun, a compelling fiddle, an unrefusable request), not as a physics of enchantment to be simulated.
- **Cross-play context**: strict local isolation. Every link stays within this play's directory.
