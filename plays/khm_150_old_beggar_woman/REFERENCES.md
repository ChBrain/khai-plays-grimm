# The Old Beggar-Woman: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Die alte Bettelfrau_ (KHM 150) as a study of the bystander's guilt: the threshold where merely watching a harm unfold becomes complicity. The production models the contrast between the boy's opening kindness (inviting the cold woman to the fire) and his failed mercy (seeing her rags catch and doing nothing), preserving the tale's unresolved, conscience-pricking fragment rather than supplying an ending.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_ (1812-1857), tale KHM 150, in the definitive 7th edition of 1857.

| Source             | Key Work / Event                   | Scope                                                                  |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Brothers Grimm** | _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_, KHM 150 | The fragmentary tale "Die alte Bettelfrau" of the beggar and the fire. |

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The woman's fate**: the tale breaks off before any resolution; the production keeps the fragment open and supplies no ending, only the narrator's questions.
- **Physics of combustion**: the rags catching alight are treated as a single state transition triggered by proximity, not a modeled fire dynamic.
- **Cross-play context**: strict local isolation. Every link stays within this play's directory.
