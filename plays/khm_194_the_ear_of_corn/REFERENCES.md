# The Ear of Corn: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Die Kornähre_ (KHM 194) as an etiological legend of squandered abundance: a once boundless blessing on the grain is forfeited through human ingratitude, and only intercession on behalf of the innocent preserves the meager ear we still harvest today. The production models the contrast between the overflowing original gift and the sparse remnant that survives divine judgment.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_ (1812-1857), tale KHM 194, German title _Die Kornähre_, in the definitive 7th edition of 1857.

| Source             | Key Work / Event                   | Scope                                                           |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Brothers Grimm** | _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_, KHM 194 | The canonical legend of why the corn-stalk bears one small ear. |

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Agronomy of the grain**: the corn-stalk and its ears are treated as a symbolic gift, ignoring the botany of cereal growth and yield.
- **Theology of providence**: the Lord's blessing, judgment, and mercy are modeled as narrative state transitions, not as a doctrine of divine governance.
