# The Rose: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Die Rose_ (KHM 203) as a children's legend in which death is figured not as a terror but as a gentle, foretold homecoming. The production models a poor woman whose youngest child is sent daily into the forest for firewood, a strange and radiant child who helps gather and carry the wood and then vanishes, and a rose given with the promise that the child will be fetched when it blooms fully. On the morning the rose opens, the child is found dead but peacefully lying in bed.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_ (1812-1857), tale KHM 203, one of the Children's Legends (Kinderlegenden) appended to the collection.

| Source             | Key Work / Event                   | Scope                                                                       |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Brothers Grimm** | _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_, KHM 203 | The children's legend of the rose, where blooming marks the appointed hour. |

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The nature of the strange child**: its origin is left unexplained. The source presents it as a radiant figure of death-as-peace, and the production refuses to name or rationalize it beyond the legend.
- **The afterlife and the moment of death**: strictly withheld. The tale shows only the peaceful body and the opened rose, never the passage itself, and the production halts there.
- **Cross-play context**: strict local isolation. Every link stays within this play's directory.
