# The Master-Thief: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Der Meisterdieb_ (KHM 192) as an illustration of consummate cunning passing three deadly trials. The production models how a long-lost peasant son, now a self-styled master of the thieving art, defeats his godfather the Count not by force but by disguise, drugged wine, a hanged man used as a decoy, and a staged Day of Judgment, only to be granted his life yet banished from the land forever.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_ (1812-1857), tale KHM 192, in the definitive 7th edition of 1857. The German title is "Der Meisterdieb".

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Ethics of theft**: thieving is staged as a tested craft, not endorsed; the moral verdict is delegated to the parents' warning and the Count's final banishment.
- **Mechanics of the sleeping draught**: the drugged wine is treated as a simple state transition into deep sleep, ignoring real pharmacology or dosage.
- **Theology of the Day of Judgment**: the staged judgment is modeled only as a deception of crawling crabs bearing lit candles, not as any genuine eschatological event.
- **Cross-play context**: strict local isolation. Every link stays within this play's directory.
