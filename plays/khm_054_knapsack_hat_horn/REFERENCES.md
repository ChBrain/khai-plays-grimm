# The Knapsack, the Hat, and the Horn: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Der Ranzen, das Hütlein und das Hörnlein_ (KHM 054) as a collision of two schemes over the same wonder-weapons: conquest against theft. The production models the tale not as a soldier's lucky tour but as a strict contest of plans, the poor brother's design to conjure armies, fire cannon, and level walls until the throne is his, set against the princess's design to coax out the secret, steal the instruments, and have him cast out. The knapsack, the hat, and the horn are the load-bearing instruments in that contest, and the outcome is decided by which scheme keeps hold of the one weapon that can still bring it all down.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmaerchen_ (1812-1857), tale KHM 054, in the definitive 7th edition of 1857.

| Source             | Key Work / Event                    | Scope                                                                                        |

| **Brothers Grimm** | _Kinder- und Hausmaerchen_, KHM 054 | The canonical tale of the wishing-cloth, the knapsack, hat, and horn, and the razed kingdom. |

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Soldier's swagger**: the brother's conquest is not bravado. It is the work of [die Eroberung des Soldaten](plan_soldiers-conquest.md), a deliberate plan whose steps win, are betrayed, and triumph in sequence.
- **The princess as mere prize**: her betrayal is given its own agency as [der Raub der Prinzessin](plan_princess-theft.md), a counter-scheme that succeeds at first and then backfires.
- **Cross-play context**: strict local isolation. Every link stays within this play's directory.
