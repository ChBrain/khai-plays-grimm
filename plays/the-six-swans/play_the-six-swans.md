# Play: The Six Swans

## Estate

[khai-plays-grimm](https://github.com/ChBrain/khai-plays-grimm): the repository that holds this production and answers for it.

## Name

The Six Swans.

## Arc

The sister enters silence in the robbers' hut the night her brothers name the price. She carries that silence from the forest floor to a king's bed to the foot of a pyre, never once setting it down. The silence is not patience: it is the instrument the enchantment requires, and every plot tightens the cost of holding it. The bargain in the wood makes the enchantment possible. The enchantment makes the condition necessary. The condition makes the silent marriage the only available shape a life can take. The silent marriage makes the slander possible and the slander makes the pyre inevitable. Only at the pyre, with six shirts on her arm and six swans dropping from the sky, does the instrument complete its work. Remove any plot and the weight shifts: cut the slander and the pyre has no cause; cut the silent marriage and the slander has no target; cut the condition and the silence is merely strange. The arc is a compression: each plot loads more onto the same unbroken silence until the moment at the fire when silence ends and the curse breaks at the same stroke.

## Triggers

**Plot 1: The Bargain in the Wood**

The first king rides out hunting and loses his way in the great forest. He wanders until dark. The witch finds him and names her price: her daughter for a wife, or he dies in the wood. He consents. She leads him out. The wedding is held. Fearing the stepmother, the king hides his seven children in the hidden forest castle and visits them by the yarn ball. The new queen bribes the servants, learns the secret, takes the yarn ball, and finds the castle. The six brothers run out to meet her, thinking it their father. She throws a shirt over each. They become swans and fly away. The girl does not run out; the queen does not know she exists. Close: the girl sees six swans fly from the courtyard. She gathers the feathers. The brothers are gone.

**Plot 2: The Enchantment**

The king comes the next day and finds only his daughter. She shows him the feathers; she tells him what she saw. He mourns. The girl, left alone that night, goes into the forest to find her brothers. She walks through the night and the next day. She enters the robbers' hut and hides under a bed. At dusk six swans fly in through the window, blow their feathers off, and strip their swan-skins. She recognises her brothers. They warn her: this is a robbers' den, and the quarter hour is nearly spent. She asks whether they can be set free. They name the condition: six years without speech or laughter, and six shirts sewn from starwort. One word ends it. The quarter hour closes. They become swans and fly out. Close: the sister resolves to free her brothers even if it costs her life.

**Plot 3: The Condition**

The sister leaves the hut. She goes to the middle of the forest and climbs a tree. She gathers starwort and begins to sew. She does not speak. She does not laugh. She sits in the tree and looks at nothing but her work. The second king's huntsmen ride into the forest and come to her tree. They call up to her; she does not answer. They climb the tree and bring her down before the king. He questions her in every language he knows. She is mute as a fish. He wraps her in his cloak, takes her to his court, and dresses her in rich garments. He asks to marry her; she cannot refuse and cannot explain. A few days later the wedding is held. Close: the silent woman is now queen at the second king's court, still sewing, still without a word.

**Plot 4: The Silent Marriage**

The second king's mother is displeased. She speaks ill of the mute queen to whoever will hear. A year passes. The queen bears a child. The queen mother takes the infant in the night and smears the sleeping queen's mouth with blood. She goes to the king and says the queen ate the child. The king cannot believe it and will not permit harm. The queen sews. A second child is born; the same is done again. The king still holds. A third child is born; the third theft and the third accusation follow. The king can no longer hold against the law. He brings the queen to judgment. She does not defend herself. She is sentenced to die by fire. Close: the sentence is set. The day of the burning is named.

**Plot 5: The Fire and the Shirts**

The day of the sentence is the last day of the sixth year. The six shirts are finished; only the left sleeve of the youngest brother's shirt is still missing. The sister is led to the pyre with the shirts on her arm. She mounts the wood. The fire is about to be lit. She looks up and sees six swans coming through the air. She throws a shirt over each as they swoop down. The swan-skins fall. Five brothers stand whole before her. The youngest has a swan's wing where his left arm should be: the sleeve was not there. Close: the enchantment is broken. The silence is broken.

**Plot 6: The Turned Verdict**

The sister speaks. She tells the second king she is innocent and falsely accused. She names what was done: the three children stolen, the blood, the accusations. The queen mother's three children are brought out alive. The second king is moved. The queen mother is bound to the stake and burned to ashes in the queen's place. Close: the verdict is turned. The king, the queen, and the six brothers live out their years.

## Stakes

Whether a woman who holds silence can be believed when she finally speaks: whether the proof she carries in her work and her living brothers outweighs a judge's sentence and a mother's word. Every plot moves it: the enchantment sets the price of silence; the condition locks it in; the silent marriage makes her vulnerable; the slander loads the scale against her; the fire and the shirts deliver the proof; the turned verdict settles it.
