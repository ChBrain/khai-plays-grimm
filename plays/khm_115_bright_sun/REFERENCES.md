# The Bright Sun Brings It to Light: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Die klare Sonne bringt's an den Tag_ (KHM 115) as a study of conscience and the impossibility of burying a crime. The production models the tale not as a string of events but as one long contest between a deliberate scheme of concealment and the truth that drives toward the light. A starving apprentice robs and kills a poor wayfarer on the road for eight farthings; the dying man calls the bright sun to witness. Years of prosperity, marriage, and family are built over the buried secret, yet a sunbeam dancing off a coffee cup loosens the murderer's tongue, a wife's pressing curiosity draws out the confession, and a friend's gossip carries it through the town until the prophecy is fulfilled in a courtroom.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_ (1812-1857), tale KHM 115, in the definitive 7th edition of 1857.

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The victim's ethnic identification**: the Grimms' source names the murdered wayfarer as a Jew. This production refuses to reproduce that gratuitous ethnic identification. The figure is encoded purely by function as [der Ermordete](persona_victim.md), the murdered wayfarer, a poor traveller robbed and killed on the road, whose spilled blood cries for justice and whose dying prophecy is the engine of the tale, never as a label or caricature.
- **The murder as spectacle**: the killing is modeled as the originating cause whose moral weight is measured against the eight farthings of plunder, not as gory detail to be dwelt upon.
- **Lucky discovery**: the murderer's exposure is not chance. It is the failure of [das verschwiegene Vorhaben](plan_bright_sun.md), undone by his own conscience, his wife's curiosity, and the unstoppable passage of a shared secret.
- **Cross-play context**: strict local isolation. Every link stays within this play's directory.
