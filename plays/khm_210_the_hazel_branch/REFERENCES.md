# The Hazel-Branch: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Die Haselrute_ (KHM 210) as a brief etiological legend that grounds a folk belief in an act of gratitude. The production models Our Lady, the mother of the Christ-child, who goes into the wood to gather strawberries for her sleeping child, is startled by an adder, takes refuge behind a hazel-bush until the snake is gone, and then blesses the hazel so that ever after a green hazel-branch shields against adders, snakes, and all that creeps on the earth.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_ (1812-1857), the children's legend KHM 210, "Die Haselrute," in the definitive 7th edition of 1857, where it stands as the very last tale of the entire collection.

| Source             | Key Work / Event                   | Scope                                                                       |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Brothers Grimm** | _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_, KHM 210 | The closing children's legend in which the hazel is blessed against snakes. |

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Theological doctrine**: the mother is staged as the figure of the legend, the mother of the Christ-child, without elaborating doctrine beyond what the source states.
- **Natural history of the adder**: the snake is treated as the narrative threat that startles and pursues, not as a biological species with modeled behavior.
- **Cross-play context**: strict local isolation. Every link stays within this play's directory.
