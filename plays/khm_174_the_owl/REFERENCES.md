# The Owl: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Die Eule_ (KHM 174) as a satire of collective panic and herd behavior. The production models how an unexamined fear of the unknown, passed unchecked from one frightened person to the next, escalates from a single servant's scream into a full civic mobilization, and how a community can inflict greater harm on itself than the imagined threat ever could by burning down its own barn to be rid of one cowering bird.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_ (1812-1857), tale KHM 174, in the definitive 7th edition of 1857.

| Source             | Key Work / Event                   | Scope                                                        |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Brothers Grimm** | _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_, KHM 174 | The comic tale of a town that burns its barn to slay an owl. |

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Ornithology of the owl**: the bird is treated as a simple harmless creature whose only acts are cowering, hissing, and crying; no biological detail of horned owls is simulated.
- **Civic procedure**: the muster of senators and burgomaster is modeled as a chain of panic and authority, not as a realistic legal or military process.
- **Cross-play context**: strict local isolation. Every link stays within this play's directory.
