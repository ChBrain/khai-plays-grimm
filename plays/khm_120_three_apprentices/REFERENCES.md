# The Three Apprentices: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Die drei Handwerksburschen_ (KHM 120) as a study of how an enforced, meaning-stripped response pattern can damn the innocent while a hidden orchestrator forces the true guilt into the open. The production models the contrast between the three apprentices, bound to three fixed phrases that read as a confession, and the richly-dressed man (the Devil) who engineers the whole ordeal to claim the soul of the real murderer.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_ (1812-1857), tale KHM 120, in the definitive 7th edition of 1857.

| Source             | Key Work / Event                   | Scope                                                                     |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Brothers Grimm** | _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_, KHM 120 | The canonical Devil's-bargain tale of the three fixed-phrase apprentices. |

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Theology of the soul**: the Devil's claim on the innkeeper's soul is treated as a narrative outcome, not a modeled metaphysics of damnation or salvation.
- **Forensics of the cellar**: the discovery of the bodies is modeled as a single state transition triggered by the white handkerchief, rather than any detailed investigation.
- **Cross-play context**: strict local isolation. Every link stays within this play's directory.
