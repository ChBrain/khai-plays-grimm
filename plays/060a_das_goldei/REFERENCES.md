# The Golden Egg: Reference

## Line of Work

Staging _Das Goldei_ (KHM 60a, withdrawn after the first edition) as a scheme of calculation undone by chance: poor broom-binder children find a dark bird that lays a golden egg each day and grow rich by selling them, until the bird asks to be taken to the goldsmith and sings that whoever eats its heart will be king and whoever eats its liver will find a purse of gold each morning; the goldsmith plots to marry the sister and roast the bird for himself, but heart and liver fall out in the roasting and are eaten by the children, on whom the prophecy comes true. The production models the tale as the reversal by which the cold buyer loses what the artless children gain. The golden egg, the heart and liver, and the gold purse are the load-bearing instruments of that design.

## Origin

Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm's _Kinder- und Hausmärchen_, first edition (1812), tale no. 60; from the second edition KHM 60 became _Die zwei Brüder_ and _Das Goldei_ passed into the notes, absent from the 1857 edition of last hand. Recorded among the tales "aus der ersten Auflage."

## Restrictions

What the production refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Roasting without gore**: the bird's death and the falling-out of heart and liver are encoded as the turning point ([die Spruchwirkung](process_spruchwirkung.md), [Herzlein und Leberlein](piece_herz-und-leber.md)) in the plain manner of the folktale, not as graphic detail.
- **Calculation, not a counter-schemer**: there is no Gegenplan. The adversary of [der Plan des Goldschmieds](plan_goldschmied.md) is chance itself, which gives the magic parts to the artless children rather than the calculating buyer.
- **Cross-play context**: strict local isolation. Every link stays within this play's directory.
